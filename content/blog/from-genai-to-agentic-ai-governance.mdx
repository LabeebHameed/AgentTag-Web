Traditional GenAI systems mostly generated text, code, or images for humans to review. Agentic AI systems go further: they can plan, call tools, trigger workflows, and affect real systems directly.

That shift is why governance matters more now than it did for earlier copilots and assistants. The core risk is no longer only “the model said something wrong.” It becomes “the model-led system did something consequential.”

### What changed

Three things changed as systems became more agentic.

- **Actionability:** agents can execute, not just suggest. 
- **Persistence:** they can operate across longer task sequences. 
- **System reach:** they can interact with multiple tools, datasets, and services. 

These changes increase productivity, but they also increase operational and governance complexity.

### Why older guardrails are not enough

Prompt-level restrictions and output moderation still matter, but they do not answer key runtime questions. They do not define what identity the agent acts under, what systems it may touch, what approvals are required, or how actions are audited after the fact.

That is why governance for agentic AI has to include identity, access controls, mandates, oversight, and logging.

### The operational view

Once an AI system can act, it starts to resemble a new kind of software operator. That means teams should evaluate it the way they would evaluate any actor with real authority: what can it do, how is that limited, what gets reviewed, and how do we investigate issues later?
